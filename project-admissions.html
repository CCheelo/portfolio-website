Admissions Bias Audit

March 2026 · Statistical Audit Power BI · Hypothesis Testing 4,839 applicants analyzed

A rigorous statistical investigation into whether university admissions data reveals gender bias. The headline finding flipped expectations — what looked like discrimination was actually driven by major-selection patterns.

Summary Overview

The Question

Does the admissions data from 4,839 university applicants show evidence of gender bias?

Methods

  • → Two-sample t-tests comparing acceptance rates by gender
  • → Chi-square test of independence (gender vs. acceptance)
  • → Stratified analysis by major to isolate confounding

The Headline Finding

At first glance, men appeared to be accepted at higher rates than women. But once results were broken down by major, the picture flipped:

  • → Majors A and B had high acceptance rates (~70%) and skewed male
  • → Majors C–F had low acceptance rates (~26%) and skewed female
  • → Within each major, acceptance rates by gender were broadly comparable

Conclusion

The apparent gender bias was a classic case of Simpson's Paradox — the headline aggregate hid the real driver (major selection). This is a textbook reminder of why stratified analysis matters when auditing for bias in any decision system.
